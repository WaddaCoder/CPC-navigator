S.96 – S.115
Appeals, Review & Revision
The appellate hierarchy runs: original decree → first appeal (S.96) → second appeal on law only (S.100) → Supreme Court (S.109). Revision (S.115) is a separate lane for jurisdictional correction, not a third appeal.
⚠ Constitutional appeals under Art. 185 lie beyond this module’s scope. The CPC ends at S.109; the Constitution takes over from there.

Think of a civil suit as a relay race. S.26 fires the starting gun (plaint). S.27–32 hand the baton to the defendant (summons & service). S.33 crosses the finish line (judgment → decree). Miss any handoff and the race is void.

Inherent Powers & Amendment
S.151 is the court’s emergency generator — when every other provision fails, this keeps the lights of justice on. It is not a backdoor around the Code; it is the residual conscience of civil procedure, available only when no other remedy exists and the ends of justice demand it.

Order I
Parties to Suits
A lawsuit is only as good as the parties in it. Join the right people and you get one judgment that settles everything. Join the wrong people — or miss someone essential — and you manufacture a second lawsuit the moment the first one ends. Order I is the guestlist rule: who must come, who may come, and what happens when the wrong people show up.

Order VI–VIII
Pleadings
Pleadings are the opening arguments in a chess game — each side lays out exactly what pieces it has and where it stands, before a single move is played. The discipline is ruthless: material facts only, no evidence, no surprises. Everything not in your pleading is conceded.

Order XIV
Framing of Issues
If a lawsuit were a trial, the issues are the charges read out before testimony begins. They define the battlefield. Every piece of evidence, every witness, every argument must attach to an issue — if it doesn’t, it doesn’t count. A badly-framed issue is a trap that springs shut on appeal.

Order XXXVII
Summary Suits
Order XXXVII is the fast lane for people holding dishonoured cheques, promissory notes, and bills of exchange. The ordinary procedure lets the defendant show up and fight as of right. Here, he has to earn his day in court — unless he can show a genuine defence, the plaintiff gets a decree without a trial.

S.75 – S.78
Commissions & Evidence-Gathering
When the witness can’t come to the court, the court sends someone to the witness. A commission is the court’s long arm — it reaches into another province, another country, or a contested piece of land, and brings back evidence that would otherwise be inaccessible. It is outsourced judicial work, done under judicial authority.

S.88 · S.89A · S.91–93
Public Interest Proceedings
Most CPC suits are private disputes between named parties. These sections open the courtroom to something bigger — disputes where multiple claimants compete for the same fund, where settlements need a judicial stamp, where a public harm needs a public remedy. The law steps out of the private law lane.

Order IX
Non-appearance & Ex Parte
Order IX is the attendance register of civil procedure. Show up and the suit proceeds. Don’t show up and the consequences depend entirely on who vanished — plaintiff, defendant, or both. An ex parte decree is the law’s way of saying: you had your invitation, you chose not to come, now face the music.

Order XI
Discovery & Inspection
Discovery is the legal equivalent of forcing your opponent to show his hand before the game is played. Interrogatories make him answer questions under oath. Document discovery makes him open his filing cabinet. Inspection lets you read what he produces. The philosophy: no surprises at trial. The truth should emerge from the evidence, not from who better concealed it.

Order XVII – XVIII
Adjournments & Hearing
Order XVII is the most abused provision in Pakistani civil litigation. It gives the court power to adjourn — and that power, used without discipline, turned the civil courts into parking lots for undecided cases. Order XVIII is what should happen instead: the hearing, the evidence, the examination, building toward a decision.

Order XX
Judgment & Decree
The judgment is the court speaking its mind. The decree is the court speaking its will. The judgment explains; the decree commands. One without the other is a body without a spine — present, but structurally useless. Order XX is the architecture of how a civil court finishes what it starts.

Order XXII
Abatement
A lawsuit is not a living thing — it does not die when its owner does. Abatement is the law’s answer to what happens when a party dies, marries, or goes insolvent mid-suit. The answer, almost always: the suit survives. The machine keeps running. Only the driver changes.

Appellate Procedure
S.96–S.115 told you the substantive law of appeals — who can appeal, on what grounds, to which court. Order XLI tells you how. Filing the memorandum, staying execution, serving the respondent, hearing the case, producing fresh evidence, remanding, deciding. The procedural rules that make the substantive right real.

Order XXI
Execution Rules
S.36–74 told you what execution is and why. Order XXI tells you how: the paperwork, the sequence, who can object, how property gets attached and sold, and when a third party can claim the furniture being carted away from their friend’s house. It is the operational manual for the enforcement machinery.

Order XXIII
Withdrawal & Compromise
Not every lawsuit needs a judge to finish it. Order XXIII is the exit ramp — the law’s recognition that parties who started a fight can also end one. But the exit ramp has tolls: withdraw wrong, and you can never re-enter that particular road.

Order XXIV – XXV
Payment into Court & Security for Costs
Two defensive procedural weapons. Payment into court is the defendant’s way of saying “here’s your money — now prove you deserve more” while simultaneously cutting off the plaintiff’s interest clock. Security for costs is the court’s way of protecting a defendant from a phantom plaintiff who files and vanishes.

Order II
Frame of Suit
Order II is the law’s answer to a plaintiff who tries to run the same race twice. The rule is simple and brutal: bring everything you have in one suit. Omit a claim, split a cause of action, or hold back a relief — and you lose it forever. You get one shot at each cause of action.

Order XXXIX
Temporary Injunctions
An injunction is the court saying “stop” before it has decided who is right. It freezes the world while the fight is still live. Order XXXIX is the most frequently invoked — and most frequently abused — procedural weapon in Pakistani civil litigation. Getting one requires three conditions. Breaking one has criminal consequences.

Order V
Service of Summons
A suit cannot proceed against a defendant who was never properly told it existed. Service of summons is the bridge between filing a suit and having the right to proceed. Get it wrong and any ex parte decree built on it is a nullity, not merely an irregularity.

Order XXXVIII
Arrest & Attachment Before Judgment
Order XXXVIII is the emergency brake before the car reaches the cliff. Where a defendant is about to abscond, remove his assets, or frustrate any eventual decree, the court can arrest him or freeze his property before judgment is even passed. Prevention rather than cure — but available only on strict statutory grounds.

Order XL
Receivers
Where both parties claim property and neither can safely hold it during litigation, the court appoints a neutral third party to manage it. The receiver is the court’s own agent — responsible to the court, not to either litigant. Mismanage the property and you answer for it personally.

Order XII – XIII
Admissions & Document Production
Order XII is the art of making the opponent admit what they know to be true before trial. Order XIII is the discipline of producing your documents at the first hearing or losing the right to rely on them. Together they strip away manufactured disputes and force the case to its real issues.

Order XVI – XIX
Witnesses & Affidavits
You cannot simply announce your witnesses on the day of hearing. Order XVI fixes the list seven days after issues are framed, funds their attendance in advance, and backs the summons with attachment powers for defaulters. Order XIX sets the rules for affidavit evidence — what it can say, and when the deponent must appear to be cross-examined.

Order III
Recognized Agents & Pleaders
You do not have to fight your own legal battles. Order III is the framework that allows someone else to stand in court for you — and determines exactly how much authority they carry, what binds you through them, and what happens when the lawyer on record receives a notice meant for you.

Order X & XV
Examination of Parties & First Hearing Disposal
Order X gives the court a scalpel at the first hearing — it can cut through the pleadings by directly questioning the parties to find out what is genuinely disputed. Order XV is the finishing move: where parties are not truly at issue, or the case can be decided on what is before the court already, the judge may pronounce judgment then and there.

Court Hierarchy & Pecuniary Jurisdiction
The CPC opens by telling you where you are in the judicial food chain and how much a court can handle. S.3 places every civil court in a hierarchy. S.4 saves all special laws. S.5 carves out revenue courts. S.6 sets the hard pecuniary ceiling that no agreement can override.

S.47
Questions for the Executing Court
S.47 is the gatekeeper of execution proceedings. Every question about how a decree is to be executed, discharged, or satisfied must be resolved by the executing court — not by a fresh suit. Open a new suit on an execution question and the court will throw it out.

Order XXVII–XXXIII
Special Parties
Not every litigant is a competent adult private citizen. Orders XXVII through XXXIII are the CPC’s special rules for parties who need extra protection or special procedure: the State, military personnel, corporations, firms, minors, persons of unsound mind, and those too poor to pay court fees. Different status, different rules.

Order XXXV
Interpleader Procedure
Order XXXV is the procedural rulebook for the goalkeeper suit we covered in S.88. A stakeholder who holds property claimed by two competing parties files an interpleader suit, pays the thing into court, and asks to be discharged. The claimants then fight it out between themselves.

Order XLIV–XLV
Pauper Appeals & Supreme Court Appeals
Justice should not be available only to those who can pay. Order XLIV extends the pauper procedure to appeals. Order XLV is the procedural gateway to the Supreme Court — a certificate, security, and the transmission of the entire record. The appellate journey ends here unless the Constitution takes over at Art. 185.

Order XXVI
Commission Procedure Rules
S.75–78 told you what commissions are and why. Order XXVI is the detailed operational manual: who executes them, how evidence is recorded, when depositions can be read without the witness appearing, how local investigation and accounts commissions work, and what happens when a foreign tribunal asks Pakistan to examine a witness here.

Order XXXIV
Mortgage Suits
A mortgage suit is always a two-decree affair — one to declare rights, one to enforce them. The preliminary decree fixes the numbers. The final decree decides fate: the mortgagor either pays and redeems, or loses the property to foreclosure or sale. Order XXXIV is the complete procedural code for all three kinds of mortgage suit.

Order XLVI – XLVII
Reference & Review
Reference and Review are two post-decree remedies that operate in different lanes. Reference sends a legal question up to the High Court before the case is decided — not to appeal, but to seek guidance on a genuine legal doubt. Review asks the same court that decided to look again — not because you disagree, but because something new has come to light or there is an error apparent on the face of the record.
